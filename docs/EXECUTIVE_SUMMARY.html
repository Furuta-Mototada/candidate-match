Candidate Match 🗳️

An AI-Powered Political Matching Platform for the Japanese National Diet
Mototada Furuta · Minerva University Capstone Project

The Problem

Japan's National Diet passes hundreds of bills each session, yet most voters have little insight into how their representatives actually vote. Traditional media coverage focuses on party labels and personalities rather than legislative behavior. Without clear, accessible data, citizens cannot easily determine which politicians truly share their views.

The Solution

Candidate Match is a web application that lets anyone discover which Diet members align with their political preferences. Inspired by Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT), the platform asks users a short series of questions about real bills — "Do you support or oppose this legislation?" — and then uses machine-learning techniques to match them with the parliament members whose voting records are most similar.

Figure 1 — End-to-end pipeline: from raw government data to personalized political insights.

Key Features

  • Adaptive Matching — asks only the most informative bills first, so users get accurate results in just a few minutes.
  • Legislation Scores — searchable database showing how every member voted on every bill.
  • Bill Clustering — groups similar legislation into policy topics using machine-learning embeddings.
  • Member Visualization — interactive latent-space map of parliament members' political positions.
  • Bilingual — full Japanese and English support.

Technology

The platform is built with SvelteKit and PostgreSQL on the web side, and a Python data pipeline that scrapes official Diet records, computes voting scores, generates bill embeddings with sentence-transformers, and clusters legislation with scikit-learn. An optional OpenAI integration produces plain-language bill summaries so users can understand complex legislation before answering.

Impact & Vision

By making legislative data accessible and personal, Candidate Match aims to strengthen democratic participation in Japan. The long-term goal is to expand coverage to local assemblies and to support civic-education initiatives that help young voters engage with politics based on policy, not just party affiliation.
